To begin with, the origin of the first ramen is unknown, but it is a fact that ramen came from an immigrant dish borrowed from China.


However, how exactly did it arrive in Japan?

1 / 12

Theory One: The Chinese Scholar

2 / 12

Early attempts to establish a history of ramen claim a scholar named Shu Shunsui brought the recipe with him when he escaped Manchu rule in China to serve as an advisor to feudal lord Tokugawa Mitsukuni.

3 / 12

Historical records state that Shu Shunsui advised Mitsukini on what to add to his udon soup to make it taste better, thus, creating the first bowl of ramen.

4 / 12

While it is true that Chinese culture heavily influenced Japanese culture at the time, a historical record of Mitsukini cooking ramen does not exist.

5 / 12

Theory Two: Rai-Rai Ken

6 / 12

The last and most plausible theory associates the origin of the ramen to a shop called Rai Rai Ken in Tokyo during the 1900s.

7 / 12

Rai Rai Ken employed Chinese workers and served a noodle dish called Shina Soba: shina for China, soba for the noodle dish already well established in Japanese cuisine.

8 / 12

Interestingly, Japan was becoming industrialized and more urbanized during this time period which helped to popularize ramen.

9 / 12

For example, Shina soba was cheap and filling, and new mechanical noodle-making machines meant that the dish was quick to make.

10 / 12

All of these conditions made ramen the perfect food to eat and ultimately integrated into Japanese modern urban life making it’s first deep roots in Japanese culture and history.

11 / 12

As far as where the word “ramen” came from, Ramen is a japanese transcription of chinese lamian, came about during the 1950s due to the disapproved use of “shina soba”

The Different Types of Ramen

Sapporo Miso Ramen

Sapporo, the capital of Hokkaido, is especially famous for its miso ramen. Sapporo miso ramen is typically topped with sweetcorn, butter, bean sprouts, finely chopped pork, and garlic, and sometimes local seafood such as scallop, squid, and crab.

kitakata Ramen

Kitakata ramen is known for its rather thick, flat, curly noodles served in a pork-and-niboshi broth. The ramen itself has a soy sauce base, and is usually topped with green onions, fish cake, char siu and bamboo shoots.

hakata Ramen

Tokyo-style ramen consists of slightly thin, curly noodles served in a soy-flavored chicken broth. Standard toppings are chopped scallion, menma, sliced pork, kamaboko, egg, nori, and spinach.
